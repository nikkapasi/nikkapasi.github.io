I wanted a cheap and convenient design for a mount that allows you to take pictures through the eyepiece of a microscope or telescope with any mobile phone.

Most Feasible Option

I chose to use this design because it required the cheapest parts and was the least difficult to construct in a short amount of time with limited money and power tools at hand. The tripod design ended up being too expensive without having to construct my own adjustable tripod and the manual option would have been a difficult construction requiring welding and tapping screw holes into metal. The manual option also did not involve an electronic component which was a skill I wanted to practice in this project.

Prototype

I connected the linear actuators and their drivers using the given adaptors.

The drivers and actuators required 5 volts to be fully powered so I used a portable battery connected through an arduino I already had to provide a safe and constant voltage.


I didn't have time to 3D print the frame, so I used a Dremel tool to cut up some scrap metal for my frame.

I then zip tied the frame together with a circular clamp to the eyepiece from my set of binoculars to test.


I fully attached both drivers to both actuators and powered them through a breadboard.

Then, I used epoxy glue and more zip ties to connect one actuator to the other for two-axis motion, and attached both to the frame to be mounted.


The completed device was mounted to the eyepiece of the binoculars inside a cardboard box. The cardboard box represents any telescope or microscope with an eyepiece attached to it.

What I struggled with

Construction and Materials

While the frame for the device was intended to be 3D printed as seen in the CAD designs, the prototype needed to be made cheaply and quickly. This resulted in lots of time spent wandering the apartment saying "maybe...this will work?" and more time making sure the Santa Barbara police didn't think I was cutting the lock off of someone's bike in Isla Vista at 2 A.M. with my Dremel tool.

Wiring

Unfortunately, the provided wires and terminal blocks connecting the drivers to their actuators would not stay connected. Also, the drivers and actuators were purchased separately from different companies, so their wire-color compatibility took lots of research to figure out. I then took a picture of the correct color-to-color connection, clipped off the terminals, and individually joined each wire together with solder and electrical tape.

Actuator Strength

When testing the actuators, I quickly realized they would not be strong enough to support the load of a phone. This might have been a design flaw caused by the phone acting as a lever on the connection between the two actuators. The problem with buying stronger actuators was the price. The image below was captured through my phone supported by my hand in the phone clamp and aligned with the actuators. Nothing was visible as the binoculars were facing a wall, but the iPhone camera was successfully aligned with the lense of the eyepiece by my device.
